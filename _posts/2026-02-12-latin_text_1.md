## <font color="#8246af"> Introduction to Nouns </font>
Latin uses **declensions** (endings) to show the role of a word in a sentence. Notice the difference between:
```
- Puella (The girl is doing the action)
- Puellam (Something is happening to the girl)
```

In Latin, nouns belong to "families" called declensions.  The most common "**Big Two**" Declensions   
- 1st Declension: Mostly *Feminine*, most end with **-a**, (example: puella - girl)  
- 2nd Declension: Mostly *Masculine* (example: amicus - friend)  
  
**<span style="background-color: #d7c6e6; border: 1px solid #8246af; padding: 2px 6px; border-radius: 3px;"> 
Tip: If a noun ends in -m, it is almost always the "Object" of the sentence (the person or thing receiving the action). 
</span>**

| Case Role                                                            | 1st Declension (Fem.) | 2nd Declension (Masc.) | Use in a Sentence                 |
| :------------------------------------------------------------------- | :-------------------: | :--------------------: | --------------------------------- |
| <span style="color: #00c389; font-weight: bold;"> Nominative </span> |        Puella         |         Amicus         | The subject (The ___ does it)     |
| <span style="color: #00c389; font-weight: bold;"> Genitive </span>   |        Puellae        |         Amici          | Possession (Of the ___)           |
| <span style="color: #00c389; font-weight: bold;"> Dative  </span>    |        Puellae        |         Amico          | Indirect object (To/for the ___)  |
| <span style="color: #00c389; font-weight: bold;"> Accusative </span> |        Puellam        |         Amicum         | Direct object (Sees the ___)      |
| <span style="color: #00c389; font-weight: bold;"> Ablative </span>   |        Puellā         |         Amico          | Prepositions (In/with/by the ___) |


* **Aqua** est frigida. (The water is cold.)
* **Puella** **aquam** portat. (The girl carries the water.)
* **Via** longa est. (The road is long.)
* **Puellae** in silvā ambulant. (The girls walk in the forest.)
* **Amicus** **puellam** amat. (The friend loves the girl.)
* **Puella** **amicum** vocat. (The friend calls the girl.)
* **Amicus** in **horto** cum **puellā** est. (The friend is in the garden with the girl.)
* **Servus** cibum parat. (The servant prepares the food.)
* **Equus** per **viam** currit. (The horse runs through the road.)
  

## <font color="#8246af"> Plurals </font>

| Number                                                             | 1st Declension (Fem) | 2nd Declension (Masc) | Role    |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------ | -------------------- | --------------------- | ------- |
| <span style="color: #00c389; font-weight: bold;"> Singular </span> | -a (Puella)          | -us (Amicus)          | Subject |
| <span style="color: #00c389; font-weight: bold;"> Singular </span> | -am (Puellam)        | -um (Amicum)          | Object  |
| <span style="color: #00c389; font-weight: bold;"> Plural </span>   | -ae (Puellae)        | -i (Amici)            | Subject |
| <span style="color: #00c389; font-weight: bold;"> Plural </span>   | -as (Puellas)        | -os (Amicos)          | Object  |

* **Amici** **equos** vident. (The friends see the horses.)
* **Servi** in horto laborant. (The servants work in the garden.)
* **Puellae** in horto ambulant. (The girls walk in the graden.)
* **Amici** **puellas** vident. (The friends see the girls.)
* **Puellae** **amicos** vocant. (The girls call the friends.)

**<span style="background-color: #d7c6e6; border: 1px solid #8246af; padding: 2px 6px; border-radius: 3px;">
Wit & Wisdom: Notice that for verbs, when the subject is plural (they), the ending usually changes from -t to -nt. It’s the Latin way of saying "there's a crowd!"
</span>**


## <font color="#8246af"> Vocabulary List </font>
- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold;"> Puella / Puellae </span>: Girl / Girls
- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold;"> Amicus / Amici </span>: Friend / Friends
- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold;"> Servus / Servi </span>: Servant / Servants
- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold;"> Equus / Equi </span>: Horse / Horses
- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold;"> Aqua </span>: Water
- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold;"> Cibus </span>: Food
- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold;"> Via </span>: Road, way
- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold;"> Hortus </span>: Garden
- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold;"> Silba </span>: Forest
- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold;"> Videt / Vident </span>: Sees / They see
- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold;"> Vocat / Vocant </span>: Calls / They call
- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold;"> Ambulat / Ambulant </span>: Walks / They walk
- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold;"> Currit / Currunt </span>: Runs / They run
- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold;"> Regina </span>: Queen
- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold;"> Nauta </span>: Sailor. Note: this is a rare *<span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold;"> masculine </span>* 1st declension word!
- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold;"> Dominus </span>: Lord, master
- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold;"> Oppidum </span>: Town. Our first **Neuter** noun, which looks like the masculine in the object form.

12) Hortus nihil habet.

2) The horses run through the forest.
3) The friends carry the water.
4) The girls call the servants.
5) The queen calls the sailors.
6) The sailor sails to the town.
7) The master praises the servant.
8) The friends are standing on the road.
9) The girl sees one friend.
10) Two servants carry water.
11) The queen calls three girls.
12) The garden has nothing.


## <font color="#8246af"> Simple Latin Quotes  </font>

- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold">  In principio creavit Deus caelum et terram. </span>   
*Genesis 1:1*, In the beginning God created heaven and earth.

- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold"> Amicus certus in re incerta cernitur. </span>  
Ennius, A sure friend is seen in an unsure matter. Or, a friend in need is a friend indeed.

- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold"> Ego sum via et veritas et vita. </span>  
John 14:6. **Breakdown**: Ego sum (I am) via (the way) et (and) veritas (the truth) et (and) vita (the life). Notice all three nouns end in -a. They are all feminine singular subjects.

- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold"> Caelum, non animum, mutant qui trans mare currunt. </span> 
By Horace, a Roman Poet. They change the sky, not their soul, who run across the sea." Caelum (sky) and animum (soul/mind) both end in -um because they are the objects—the things being changed.

- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold"> Da mihi basia mille, deinde centum...  </span>   
Give me a thousand kisses, then a hundred...
