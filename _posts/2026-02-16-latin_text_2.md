From "The girl sees the friends" to "The girl sees **her** friends": the genitive case.

## <font color="#8246af"> The genitive cases </font>
First, for the two declension 
```
1st Declension (-ae): Puellae amicus (The girl's friend)
2nd Declension (-i): Amici equus (The friend's horse)
```

- 1st Declension (Genitive Singular: -ae)

    Cibus **puellae**: The food of the girl (The girl's food).

    Aqua **silvae**: The water of the forest.

    Amica **reginae**: The friend of the queen (The queen's friend).

- 2nd Declension (Genitive Singular: -i)

    Hortus **amici**: The garden of the friend (The friend's garden).

    Cibus **equi**: The food of the horse (The horse's food).

    Servus **domini**: The servant of the master.

<span style="background-color: #d7c6e6; border: 1px solid #8246af; padding: 2px 6px; border-radius: 3px; font-weight: bold;"> A helpful trick: Whenever you see -ae (on a 1st declension noun) or -i (on a 2nd declension noun) and it isn't the subject, try putting "of" in front of it. </span>

<span style="color: #00c389; font-weight: bold;"> Literary Examples </span>

-  <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold;"> Amor pecuniae </span >: The love of money.

-  <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold;">Gloria Romanorum</span>: The glory of the Romans.

-  <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold;"> Agnum Dei</span>: The Lamb of God (Agnus = Lamb, Deus = God).

<span style="color: #00c389; font-weight: bold;"> Let's combine everything: Subjects, Objects, and now the Owners (Genitive). </span>

- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold;"> Servus equum amici ducit. </span>
**Translation**: The servant leads the friend's horse.
**Logic**: Servus (Subject) + equum (Object) + amici (Owner).

- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold;">Puellae aquam silvae vident. </span>
**Translation**: The girls see the water of the forest.

- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold;"> Amicus reginae in horto ambulat. </span>
**Translation**: The friend of the queen walks in the garden.

- <span style="color: #407ec9; font-weight: bold;"> Dominus cibum servorum parat. </span>  (Note: -orum is the plural Genitive "of the servants").
**Translation**: The master prepares the food of the servants.

## <font color="#8246af"> Practice Exercises & Answers </font>
<span style="font-size: 18px; color: #00c389; font-weight: bold;"> Questions </span>

1) Regina equos amicorum videt.
2) Puella cibum equi portat.
3) Amicos reginae amamus.
4) Aquam puellae portatis.
5) Servi cibum equorum parant.

<span style="font-size: 18px; color: #00c389; font-weight: bold;"> Answers </span>

1) The queen sees the horse of the friends.
2) The girl carries the horse's food.
3) **We** love the queen's friends.
4) **You** all carry the girl's water.
5) The servants prepare the horses' food.

### The Concept of Zero
The Romans did not have a numeral for zero. To express "nothing" or "none," they used:
* **Nihil:** Nothing (Noun). *Example: Nihil video (I see nothing).*
* **Nullus, -a, -um:** None / No [adj]. *Example: Nullus amicus adest (No friend is present).*
* **Nulla:** Used by later medieval mathematicians to represent the digit zero.

### Counting to 100 (Tens)
Counting by tens uses the suffix **-ginta** (similar to "-ty").

* 20: **Viginti**
* 30: **Triginta**
* 40: **Quadraginta**
* 50: **Quinquaginta**
* 60: **Sexaginta**
* 70: **Septuaginta**
* 80: **Octoginta**
* 90: **Nonaginta**
* 100: **Centum**
